When buyers compare two homes in two states, they anchor on the sticker price and the mortgage rate and almost never on the property tax, which is the one cost that never goes away and never gets paid off. A $400,000 home in Alabama carries about $1,500 a year in property tax. The identical price in New Jersey carries about $6,700. Over a 30-year mortgage that gap is more than $150,000, and it keeps running long after the loan is retired.

## Rate versus dollars: two different questions

The table sorts cleanly by rate, but rate alone can mislead, because it ignores what homes actually cost in each state.

- **Hawaii** has the lowest rate in the country at 0.31%, yet its median home value is the highest in the nation, so the real annual bill lands in the mid-thousands, not the low hundreds the rate implies.
- **Texas** looks punishing at 1.25% and has no assessment cap for non-homestead property, but its home prices sit below the coastal states, which softens the blow, and the lack of a state income tax offsets it for many households.
- **Alabama and West Virginia** are the genuine bargains: low rates sitting on low-to-moderate home values produce some of the smallest property tax bills in the country.

The lesson for anyone comparing an offer in two states is to run the rate against the actual price in each place, not against a national average. ## What moves your real rate off the state average

Three levers explain almost every gap between the state figure and your actual bill:

1. **Local mill rates.** Property tax is overwhelmingly local. Within one state, a high-spending school district can carry a rate double that of a rural county an hour away. The state number is just the population-weighted middle of that range.
2. **Assessment caps.** States like California (Prop 13, 2% cap), Florida (3% homestead cap), and Nevada (3% cap) limit how fast your taxable value can climb. In fast-appreciating markets, long-time owners pay dramatically less than the state's effective rate, and recent buyers pay more.
3. **Homestead exemptions.** Most states shave a fixed amount off the taxable value of an owner-occupied primary residence. These exemptions matter most on lower-priced homes, where a $50,000 exemption is a large share of the value.

## Why this number belongs in your buying decision

Property tax is the cost buyers most often underestimate and lenders most often escrow, which means a high-tax state raises your monthly payment before you have furnished a single room. When you are weighing a relocation or choosing between metro areas, treat the effective rate as a permanent line item, not a footnote, the same way you would treat the interest rate. The [mortgage tax benefits guide](/mortgage-tax-benefits/) covers the partial federal offset (the SALT deduction, capped and only useful if you itemize), and the [first-time homebuyer programs guide](/first-time-homebuyer-programs/) covers assistance that can ease the upfront side.
