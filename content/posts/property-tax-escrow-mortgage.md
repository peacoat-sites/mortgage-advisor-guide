You got your first mortgage statement in the mail, scanned down to the payment breakdown, and stopped cold. The number was higher than what you were quoted at closing. Not by a little, either. There's a line item called "escrow" that's eating up a few hundred dollars every month, and nobody explained what it actually is or whether you can get out of it. I've seen this exact moment of confusion play out hundreds of times across my career. The good news is it's not a mistake on the statement. The complicated news is that escrow is one of those things lenders love to gloss over because it makes the total payment feel bigger during sales conversations.

Let me break it down in a way that actually makes sense.

## What Property Tax Escrow Actually Is (And Why Your Lender Loves It)

An escrow account, in the context of your mortgage, is a holding account managed by your lender or loan servicer. Every month, a portion of your payment gets deposited into it. When your property tax bill comes due, the servicer pays it directly from that account on your behalf.

The same account typically covers homeowner's insurance premiums. You'll hear these two items bundled together under the acronym PITI: principal, interest, taxes, and insurance. That "T" and "I" at the end are your escrow components.

Here's the part that surprises most people: your lender usually isn't doing this as a favor to you. They're doing it to protect their collateral. Your lender has a legal and financial interest in the property until you pay off the loan. If your property taxes go unpaid, the county can place a tax lien that supersedes your mortgage. If your homeowner's insurance lapses and the house burns down, the lender's security is gone. Escrow is their way of making sure neither of those things happens, regardless of whether you remember to write the check.

That's not cynicism. It's just how the system works, and understanding the "why" helps you ask smarter questions.

This is where things get genuinely confusing, and where I've seen borrowers get blindsided most often.

Your servicer estimates what your property taxes and insurance will cost over the next 12 months, divides that by 12, and adds it to your monthly payment. Straightforward enough. But federal law, specifically the Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA), allows your servicer to also maintain a "cushion" in the escrow account of up to two months' worth of projected escrow payments. That buffer is sitting there as a reserve against shortfalls.

So your escrow payment isn't just one-twelfth of your annual tax bill. It's that, plus one-twelfth of your insurance premium, plus a portion that builds toward that two-month cushion. If your estimated annual tax bill is $4,800 and your insurance runs $1,200, you're looking at $500/month in escrow before that cushion even enters the picture.

The number that catches people off guard is usually the initial deposit at closing. You may have paid two to three months of escrow upfront as a "prepaid" expense on your Closing Disclosure. That money seeds the account. Then your monthly contributions continue building it. It feels like you're paying twice, and in a sense, you are getting the account funded from multiple directions at once. This is completely legal and extremely common. It's also detailed on the [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/) website if you want to see the regulatory framework spelled out.

## The Annual Escrow Analysis: What It Is and What It Triggers

Once a year, your servicer is required to perform what's called an escrow analysis. They look at what you paid in and what actually went out for taxes and insurance, then recalculate what the coming year's obligations are likely to be.

Two outcomes are possible.

If more money went in than went out, you have a surplus. If that surplus exceeds one month's escrow payment, your servicer is required to refund it. You'll get a check, or a credit against your next payment, depending on the servicer. This feels like a win. And it is, sort of. But what it actually reflects is that your taxes or insurance came in lower than estimated, or that your cushion got padded higher than RESPA allows.

If less money went in than went out, you have a shortage. This is the one that stings. Your servicer will either ask you to pay the shortage in a lump sum or, more commonly, spread it across the next 12 months by increasing your monthly payment. If your property was reassessed upward and your tax bill jumped by $900, that's $75 extra per month for the next year to cover the shortfall. When those adjusted statements arrive, they catch people off guard. I've had clients call me thinking the bank made an error. It's not an error. It's the escrow analysis doing exactly what it's supposed to do.

The important thing to do when you receive your escrow analysis statement is actually read it. Look at the projected disbursements, compare them to last year's actual disbursements, and check whether your county has reassessed your home recently. If the numbers look wrong to you, you can dispute them. Your servicer has a process for this.

## How to Tell If You're Overpaying Into Escrow (And What To Do About It)

Your servicer isn't always perfectly accurate with these estimates. They have to forecast a full year out, and property taxes in particular can move in ways that are hard to predict before a reassessment is finalized.

Here's a simple way to check whether your escrow is being calculated reasonably:

**Step 1:** Get your most recent property tax bill from your county assessor's website or your records. Use the actual amount due, not an estimate.

**Step 2:** Get your homeowner's insurance declaration page. Find the annual premium.

**Step 3:** Add those two numbers together. Divide by 12. That's roughly your baseline monthly escrow obligation.

**Step 4:** Multiply your monthly escrow by 12. Subtract your step-three baseline number. The difference, divided by 12, tells you how much of your monthly payment is going toward the RESPA cushion rather than direct obligations.

**Step 5:** If the cushion portion seems to exceed two months of your baseline monthly amount, you may have an inflated escrow. Request a review in writing from your servicer.

**Step 6:** Keep records of everything. Any request you make, note the date, the rep's name if you called, and what was agreed to.

## Can You Remove Escrow From Your Mortgage?

Yes, but with conditions. It's called "waiving escrow" or getting an "escrow waiver," and not every borrower qualifies.

Most conventional lenders will consider waiving escrow if you have at least 20% equity in the property, a strong payment history, and sometimes a credit score above a certain threshold. Some lenders charge a fee for the waiver, often between 0.125% and 0.25% of the loan amount, because from their perspective, they're taking on more risk by trusting you to pay the tax bill yourself.

FHA loans are a different story. HUD requires escrow for FHA loans for the life of the loan in most cases. You can't waive it. VA loans have similar restrictions depending on the lender. If you want to understand your specific loan type's requirements, [HUD-approved housing counselors](https://www.hud.gov/i_want_to/talk_to_a_housing_counselor) can review your situation with you at no cost and give you guidance that's actually tailored to your circumstances.

What most people don't realize is that even if you successfully waive escrow, the discipline of setting money aside is still your responsibility. Your county tax bill doesn't care whether you have an escrow account. Missing a property tax payment can result in penalties, interest, and eventually a tax lien. I've seen borrowers waive escrow, spend the extra monthly cash flow, and then scramble to cover a $6,000 tax bill in January. If you waive escrow, treat yourself as your own escrow servicer: open a separate savings account, deposit your monthly equivalent, and don't touch it.

## A Comparison: Escrow Account vs. Managing Taxes and Insurance Yourself

| Factor | Escrow Account | Self-Managed |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly payment predictability | Adjusts annually based on analysis | Stable, but you handle lump sums |
| Risk of missed tax payment | Very low (lender manages it) | Higher, depends on your discipline |
| Cash flow flexibility | Lower (money is held) | Higher (you hold the funds) |
| Interest earned on reserves | None (escrow doesn't earn interest in most states) | Yes, if funds are in a high-yield savings account |
| Eligibility | Open to most borrowers | Typically requires 20%+ equity; not available on FHA loans |
| Lender fee to waive | Sometimes (0.125%–0.25% of loan) | None after waiver is approved |
| Complexity for borrower | Low | Moderate to high |

The honest take is that for most first-time buyers or anyone with a tight monthly budget, escrow is the safer option. The risk of missing a property tax payment far outweighs the marginal benefit of holding your own reserves. For experienced homeowners with strong financial habits and meaningful equity, waiving escrow and investing that float can make sense. Just go in with eyes open.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why did my mortgage payment increase if my interest rate didn't change?

Your interest rate only affects the principal and interest portion of your payment. If your escrow payment increased, it's because your property taxes or homeowner's insurance premiums went up, or your annual escrow analysis revealed a shortage. Check your escrow analysis statement, which your servicer is required to send you. It will break down exactly what changed and why.

### What happens to my escrow balance if I sell the house or refinance?

If you sell, your escrow balance is typically refunded to you at closing after all outstanding obligations are settled. The timeline varies but is usually within 30 days of loan payoff. If you refinance with a new lender, the old escrow account is closed and refunded, and a new escrow account is established with the new loan, which is why you'll often see escrow prepayments as a closing cost on a refinance.

### Can I use my escrow account to pay supplemental property taxes?

Usually not automatically. Supplemental tax bills, which are common after a home is purchased and reassessed at the sale price, are often not anticipated in your initial escrow setup. You may receive a supplemental bill directly from your county that your servicer didn't plan for. Read any tax mail that comes to your address, even if you think escrow "handles all of that." It doesn't always catch every bill.

### Is the money in my escrow account earning interest?

In most states, no. Federal law does not require lenders to pay interest on escrow funds, and most don't. A small number of states, including California and New York, have laws requiring interest to be paid on escrow balances, but the rate is typically minimal. If you're in one of those states, check your loan agreement to confirm whether it applies to your situation.

### What should I do if I think my escrow analysis is wrong?

Request an escrow review in writing from your servicer. Include your actual tax bill and insurance declarations as supporting documentation. Servicers make errors, particularly during periods of rising home values when assessed values are catching up to market values unevenly. You have the right to contest the calculation. If you're not getting a clear response, the CFPB has a complaint process that tends to get servicer attention quickly.
