You run your own business, your accountant is brilliant at minimizing your taxable income, and you just got turned down for a mortgage. Sound familiar? This trap catches self-employed borrowers every single time. The same tax strategy that saves you thousands in April becomes the thing that kills your loan application in June. After 16 years underwriting loans, I can tell you that self-employed borrowers aren't riskier than W-2 employees. They just need to understand a completely different rulebook before walking into a lender's office.

## Why Lenders Look at Self-Employment Income Differently

When a salaried employee applies for a mortgage, the underwriter has one job: verify that the pay stub matches the tax return and confirm it'll keep coming. Clean. Simple. Done.

When you're self-employed, the underwriter's real job is figuring out what you actually earned after every legitimate business deduction, depreciation write-off, and Schedule C adjustment. Those two numbers can look wildly different. I've seen a borrower show $180,000 on a profit-and-loss statement and $62,000 on a tax return. The lender uses the $62,000. That's what determines how much house you can afford.

Lenders define self-employed as owning 25% or more of a business or receiving income primarily through 1099s rather than W-2s. Freelancers, sole proprietors, LLC owners, S-corp shareholders, and independent contractors all fall into this category. The documentation requirements and income calculation methods depend on your specific business structure.

The core issue is stability. Lenders want to see that your income isn't a one-year anomaly. Two years of self-employment history is the standard minimum for most conventional loans. Some lenders will consider one year if the income increased and you've got a strong track record in the same field, but that's rare.

Stated income loans are dead. They mostly disappeared after 2008, and for good reason. Today, you're going to document everything. The more organized you are before you apply, the faster this moves.

Here's what most lenders will require for self-employed borrowers:

**Tax Returns**
- Two years of personal federal tax returns (all schedules included)
- Two years of business tax returns if you operate as an S-corp or partnership (Form 1120-S or Form 1065)
- All K-1 schedules if you receive pass-through income

**Income Verification**
- A year-to-date profit-and-loss statement, often required to be prepared by a CPA
- Business bank statements for the past 12 to 24 months
- Personal bank statements for the past 2 to 3 months

**Business Documentation**
- Proof the business exists: a business license, CPA letter, DBA registration, or professional license
- Signed 4506-C form authorizing the lender to pull your tax transcripts directly from the IRS

The [home loan application checklist](/home-loan-application-checklist/) on this site can help you organize everything in one place so nothing slips through.

Here's something loan officers sometimes skip: the lender will cross-reference your business bank statements against your tax return. If your business shows $200,000 in deposits but your Schedule C reports $80,000 in income, the underwriter will want to understand the gap. Have a clear explanation ready, and make sure your CPA can back it up.

## How Lenders Calculate Your Qualifying Income

This is where the math gets complicated, and where a lot of borrowers get blindsided.

For a sole proprietor or single-member LLC filing a Schedule C, lenders start with your net profit and add back certain non-cash deductions. Depreciation is the big one. Write off $15,000 in depreciation on equipment, and that $15,000 gets added back to your qualifying income because you didn't actually spend that cash. Business mileage deductions, home office deductions, and one-time business losses get added back too. The result is called your adjusted income or qualifying income.

For S-corp owners, lenders look at your W-2 wages from the business plus your proportional share of business income (from the K-1), then add back depreciation and subtract business losses. It's complicated. This is why having a CPA who understands mortgage qualification actually matters. Not every accountant thinks about mortgages when they're filing your return.

The lender then averages your qualifying income over two years. If you earned $90,000 in year one and $130,000 in year two, your qualifying income is $110,000 per year. But here's what most people miss: if your income declined from year one to year two, most lenders will use the lower year's income, not the average. A drop in earnings is a red flag, and underwriters treat it conservatively.

Your qualifying income determines your maximum loan amount through your [debt-to-income ratio](/debt-to-income-ratio-for-mortgage/). Most conventional loans want your total monthly debt payments to stay below 43% to 45% of your gross monthly qualifying income. That ratio is based on the averaged, adjusted number, not your gross revenue.

## Loan Options for Self-Employed Borrowers

You've got more options than you might think, though each one has tradeoffs.

**Conventional Loans**

These are the most common and typically offer the best rates for borrowers with strong credit and documented income. [Conventional loan requirements](/conventional-loan-requirements/) generally include a minimum credit score around 620, though you'll get meaningfully better rates at 740 and above. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac both have guidelines specifically addressing self-employment income calculation. The [Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA)](https://www.fhfa.gov/) oversees both entities and publishes updated guidelines that lenders follow when underwriting these loans.

**FHA Loans**

FHA allows self-employment income and is generally more flexible on credit scores and debt-to-income ratios. You can qualify with a score as low as 580 with a 3.5% down payment. The tradeoff is mortgage insurance, which stays on FHA loans for the life of the loan if your down payment is under 10%. Review the full breakdown of [FHA loan requirements and benefits](/fha-loan-requirements-and-benefits/) before assuming FHA is your best path.

**Bank Statement Loans**

These are non-QM (non-qualified mortgage) products offered by specialty lenders. Instead of tax returns, they use 12 or 24 months of bank statements to calculate income. This can be a genuine solution for high-earning self-employed borrowers whose tax strategies dramatically reduce their reported income. The catch: you'll pay for the flexibility. Rates on bank statement loans typically run 1% to 2% higher than conventional rates, and down payment requirements are usually at least 10%, often 20%.

**Jumbo Loans**

If you're borrowing above the conforming loan limit (which the FHFA adjusts annually), you're in jumbo territory. [Jumbo loan requirements](/jumbo-loan-requirements/) tend to be stricter: higher credit scores, more cash reserves, and often a full 20% down payment. Self-employed jumbo borrowers frequently need 12 to 24 months of reserves on top of their down payment and closing costs.

## A Practical Step-by-Step Approach for Self-Employed Borrowers

This is the order of operations I'd recommend to any self-employed client before they start shopping for a home.

**Step 1: Pull your last two years of tax returns and do the math yourself.**

Add back depreciation and any obvious non-cash deductions to your net income on Schedule C. Average the two years. If that number supports the mortgage you need, you're in reasonable shape. If it doesn't, you need a plan before you apply.

**Step 2: Check your credit.**

Self-employed borrowers often have more complex credit profiles. A score below 700 will limit your options. [Understanding what credit score you need for mortgage approval](/credit-score-for-mortgage-approval/) is foundational.

**Step 3: Talk to a CPA before your next tax filing.**

If you're planning to buy in the next 12 to 24 months, tell your accountant. There may be ways to preserve more qualifying income while still managing your tax burden effectively. This is a delicate balance, and every situation is different. Professional advice matters here.

**Step 4: Get pre-qualified, then get pre-approved.**

Pre-qualification is a conversation. Pre-approval is a commitment with documentation reviewed. For self-employed borrowers, the gap between the two is significant. Learn [how to get pre-qualified for a mortgage](/how-to-get-pre-qualified-for-mortgage/) first, but know that sellers will expect a full pre-approval letter.

**Step 5: Prepare a business summary letter.**

Write a clear, factual one-page explanation of your business: what it does, how long you've been operating, your primary revenue sources, and why your income is stable or growing. This goes in your loan file and helps the underwriter connect the dots. [Freddie Mac's home buyer resources](https://myhome.freddiemac.com/) also offer useful guidance on what lenders look for in non-traditional income situations.

**Step 6: Don't change your business structure during the application.**

Switching from a sole proprietorship to an LLC, or from an LLC to an S-corp, mid-application creates documentation nightmares. Wait until after closing.

Getting a mortgage as a self-employed borrower is absolutely achievable. Millions of business owners do it every year. What separates the ones who close successfully from the ones who get denied is preparation: understanding how income is calculated before applying, keeping documentation organized, and working with a CPA and loan officer who have real experience with self-employment files. Start the process at least 12 to 24 months before you plan to buy, and you'll have the time to make strategic decisions rather than reactive ones.
