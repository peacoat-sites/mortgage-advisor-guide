You're sitting across from a loan officer, the stack of closing documents is three inches thick, and somewhere near the top of the loan estimate is a line that says "Discount Points: 1.000 ($4,200)." The loan officer mentions it briefly, says it'll lower your rate, and moves on. If you didn't stop them right there and ask exactly what that means for your specific loan, you may have just agreed to pay thousands of dollars without fully understanding what you were buying. I've seen that happen more times than I can count.

Mortgage points are one of those concepts that sound simple on the surface but have real financial teeth. Get them right and you can save a meaningful amount over the life of your loan. Get them wrong and you've handed your lender a premium payment that you'll never recoup.

## What Mortgage Points Actually Are

There are two kinds of points, and the industry doesn't always do a great job of keeping them separate in conversation.

**Discount points** are prepaid interest. You pay money upfront at closing to buy down your interest rate. One point equals 1% of your loan amount. On a $420,000 loan, one point costs $4,200. In exchange, your lender reduces your interest rate, typically by 0.25% per point, though that relationship is not fixed. It depends on the lender, the loan type, current market conditions, and sometimes even the day of the week.

**Origination points** are a fee the lender charges to process and originate your loan. They're not buying you a lower rate. They're just a cost of doing business, dressed up in the same "points" language. Always ask which type is on your loan estimate and what each one is doing for you.

The [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/) requires lenders to disclose both types clearly on your Loan Estimate under Section A of the closing costs. If you're not sure where to look, that's your starting point. Pull that document out and find the line item before you agree to anything.

## How the Math Actually Works: The Break-Even Calculation

This is the only number that really matters when you're deciding whether to pay points. The break-even point is how long you need to stay in the loan before the monthly savings from your reduced rate cover what you paid upfront.

Here's how to calculate it:

**Step 1:** Get two loan quotes from your lender: one with points, one without. Make sure the only difference is the rate and the points cost. Everything else should be identical.

**Step 2:** Calculate your monthly payment for each scenario. Even a rough estimate using a mortgage calculator works here.

**Step 3:** Subtract the lower monthly payment (the points scenario) from the higher one to find your monthly savings.

**Step 4:** Divide the upfront cost of the points by the monthly savings.

That result is your break-even month.

**Example:** Say you're borrowing $400,000. Without points, your rate is 7.0% and your principal and interest payment is $2,661. Your lender offers to drop the rate to 6.75% if you pay one point ($4,000) at closing. At 6.75%, your payment becomes $2,594. That's a monthly savings of $67.

$4,000 divided by $67 = roughly 60 months, or 5 years.

If you stay in that loan for more than 5 years, the points paid off. If you refinance or sell before that, you lost money on the deal.

Before you go further on the math, it helps to have a solid read on your total affordability picture. If you haven't run the numbers on [how much house you can actually afford](/how-much-house-can-i-afford/), do that first, because the size of your loan changes everything about this calculation.

## When Paying Points Makes Sense (and When It Doesn't)

Paying points isn't inherently good or bad. It's a bet on time. The longer you stay in the loan, the better the bet looks.

**Pay points if:**

- You're buying your forever home, or at least plan to stay 7-plus years
- You have the cash to pay them without draining your reserves (leaving yourself cash-poor at closing is a serious risk)
- Rates are elevated and you want to lock in a lower effective cost for the long haul
- You're on a fixed income or tight budget and need the lowest possible monthly payment

**Don't pay points if:**

- You're buying a starter home you'll likely outgrow in 3-4 years
- You're in a rate environment where refinancing in 12-24 months seems likely
- Your cash reserves are thin, because 3-6 months of housing expenses in savings is the baseline you want going into any mortgage
- You're already stretching to afford closing costs

I've seen buyers pay two points to get a lower rate and then refinance 18 months later when rates dropped. They essentially donated money to their lender twice. The decision to buy points is also tied to whether you're choosing a fixed or adjustable rate product. If you're considering an ARM, read through the details on [fixed vs. adjustable rate mortgages](/fixed-vs-adjustable-rate-mortgage/) before you add points into the equation, because the math changes significantly when your rate can move.

## Comparing Points Across Different Loan Types

Not all loan products treat points the same way, and this distinction matters.

**Conventional loans** give you the most flexibility. You can buy points, and they're often fully negotiable. The [Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA)](https://www.fhfa.gov/) sets conforming loan limits, and any loan within those limits will generally have a robust market for point buydowns.

**FHA loans** allow points too, but here's the catch: FHA loans already carry mortgage insurance premiums that don't go away until you've paid down enough equity or refinance. You need to factor that MIP cost into your break-even calculation, not just the rate difference. If you're weighing whether FHA is even the right fit for your situation, the full breakdown of [FHA loan requirements and benefits](/fha-loan-requirements-and-benefits/) is worth reading before you talk points with a loan officer.

**VA loans** allow discount points and they're tax deductible in most cases, but VA lenders are not allowed to charge origination points in the traditional sense under VA guidelines. This makes the points conversation cleaner: anything you're paying upfront is genuinely buying you a lower rate.

**Jumbo loans** can behave differently still. Lenders have more discretion with jumbo pricing, so the rate reduction you get per point may be larger or smaller than on a conforming loan. Never assume a standard conversion.

| Loan Type | Points Allowed | MIP/PMI Factor | Negotiability |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conventional | Yes | PMI may apply | High |
| FHA | Yes | MIP adds complexity | Moderate |
| VA | Discount points only | No PMI | Moderate |
| Jumbo | Yes | No MIP/PMI | Variable |

## The Tax Angle on Mortgage Points

Points can be tax deductible, but the rules have conditions most borrowers don't know about.

For a home purchase, discount points paid on your primary residence are generally fully deductible in the year you paid them, provided the points are a standard charging practice in your area and were not paid in place of fees that would normally be itemized separately. That's IRS language, and it matters.

For a refinance, points are not deductible all at once. You have to deduct them over the life of the loan. If you pay $3,000 in points on a 30-year refinance, you're deducting $100 per year. Not as dramatic.

Two important caveats. You have to itemize deductions to claim this benefit at all, and since the 2017 tax law raised the standard deduction significantly, fewer homeowners itemize than before. And this is a tax situation, not a mortgage situation, so please talk to a CPA or tax advisor about your specific circumstances before making any decisions based on deductibility.

## Seller-Paid Points and Temporary Buydowns

Here's a strategy that's gotten more attention as rates have climbed: asking the seller to pay your points.

In a buyer's market or a negotiation where the seller is motivated, you can ask them to pay closing costs, including points, as a concession. The seller reduces their net proceeds, but you get a lower rate without spending your own cash. Your break-even calculation still applies, but the cost is coming from the other side of the table.

A variation on this is the **temporary buydown**, often structured as a 2-1 buydown or a 3-2-1 buydown. In a 2-1 buydown, your rate is 2% below the note rate in year one, 1% below in year two, and then settles at the note rate from year three forward. The cost of the buydown is paid upfront, often by the seller or builder, and placed in an escrow account that subsidizes your payments.

These are not permanent rate reductions. When year three hits, your payment goes up. Make sure you can afford the full payment, not just the buydown payment, before signing.
