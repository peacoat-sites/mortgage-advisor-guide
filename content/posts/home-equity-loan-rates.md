You've got $80,000 in home equity and a kitchen that looks like it hasn't been updated since the Clinton administration. A home equity loan sounds like the obvious move. But here's what most articles on this topic skip: the rate you see advertised and the rate you'll actually get can be worlds apart, and the gap between them can cost you thousands over the life of the loan. Before you call a lender, you need to understand what drives home equity loan rates, what makes them different from other borrowing options, and exactly what to watch for in the fine print.

## What Home Equity Loan Rates Actually Are (And What Moves Them)

A home equity loan is a second mortgage. You borrow a lump sum against the equity in your home, repay it at a fixed interest rate over a set term, and your house is on the line if you don't. That last part matters more than most borrowers want to think about.

The rate you're quoted is shaped by several overlapping factors:

**Your credit score** carries enormous weight. Lenders typically tier rates in bands, and the difference between a 680 and a 760 score can be a full percentage point or more on the same loan amount. That's not a rounding error over a 10-year repayment term.

**Your combined loan-to-value ratio (CLTV)** is the one borrowers most frequently underestimate. This isn't just your home equity loan divided by your home's value. It's your first mortgage balance plus the new loan, divided by the appraised value. Most lenders cap CLTV at 80% to 85%. Push toward that ceiling and your rate goes up. Stay well below it and you'll be in a better negotiating position.

**The broader interest rate environment** sets the floor. Home equity loan rates are fixed, but they're benchmarked loosely against the 10-year Treasury yield and influenced by the same Federal Reserve policy decisions that move mortgage rates. When the Fed tightens, home equity rates rise. There's no magic immunity just because you're borrowing against your house.

**The lender's own cost structure and appetite for this product** also plays a role. Credit unions, regional banks, and national lenders all price home equity loans differently. I've seen clients get meaningfully different quotes on the same day from different institutions with identical profiles.

These two products get lumped together constantly, and conflating them is one of the most common mistakes I see. They are not interchangeable.

A home equity loan gives you a lump sum at a fixed rate. Your monthly payment is identical from month one to month 120 (or whatever your term is). Predictable, simple, good for a defined project with a known cost.

A home equity line of credit (HELOC) is a revolving line at a variable rate. During the draw period, typically 10 years, you borrow what you need and pay interest only or interest plus principal. After that, the repayment period kicks in, and your payment can jump significantly.

The critical distinction on rates: HELOCs are almost always variable, tied to the prime rate, which moves with the federal funds rate. Home equity loans are fixed. Right now, if rates are elevated, a fixed home equity loan locks you in at a known cost. If rates drop significantly, you're stuck unless you refinance. A HELOC floats down with the market, but it also floats up, and there's no ceiling on what "up" means short of the lifetime cap buried in your loan documents.

For someone borrowing $50,000 to fund a specific renovation with a firm budget, the fixed certainty of a home equity loan is usually worth the slight premium over a HELOC's initial rate. For someone who needs flexible access over several years and can tolerate payment variability, a HELOC may fit better. The right answer depends on your situation, not on which product has the lower number in an advertisement.

## What Lenders Don't Volunteer About the Rate You're Quoted

Advertised rates assume a best-case borrower: excellent credit, significant equity, stable income, low CLTV, and often a direct deposit or existing relationship with the lender. If you're not that borrower in every dimension, your rate will be higher.

What lenders tend to gloss over:

**Rate discounts with conditions attached.** Many lenders advertise a rate that includes a 0.25% or 0.50% discount for setting up autopay from a checking account at the same institution. Cancel that autopay later, or let the account close, and your rate adjusts upward. Read the autopay terms carefully.

**Prepayment penalties.** Less common than they used to be, but they still exist on some home equity products. If you pay off the loan early, you could owe a fee. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's [home loan resources](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/) are a useful starting point for understanding what lenders are legally required to disclose and when.

**Closing costs.** Home equity loans typically carry closing costs of 2% to 5% of the loan amount, including origination fees, an appraisal, title search, and recording fees. Some lenders advertise "no closing cost" loans but roll those costs into a higher rate. You're paying either way. Ask for a loan estimate that itemizes every fee before you agree to anything.

**The appraisal risk.** Your rate offer is based on an estimated value until the appraisal is done. If your home appraises lower than expected, your CLTV jumps, and your rate may change. Or your approval amount drops. Plan for this possibility.

## How to Actually Compare Home Equity Loan Rates

Don't compare APRs from three different lender websites and call it a day. That's barely a starting point.

Here's a more useful approach:

1. **Pull your credit reports first.** Check all three bureaus at AnnualCreditReport.com before you apply anywhere. Dispute errors before rate shopping. One incorrect derogatory item can push you into a worse pricing tier.

2. **Estimate your CLTV before talking to lenders.** Get a rough current value from a local real estate agent or an online estimator, then do the math: (current mortgage balance + desired loan amount) divided by home value. If that number is above 80%, you'll likely face higher rates and stricter qualification.

3. **Apply to at least three lenders within a 14-day window.** Credit scoring models treat multiple mortgage-related inquiries within a short window as a single inquiry. Shopping around doesn't hurt your score the way people fear, as long as you compress the timeline.

4. **Request a Loan Estimate from each lender.** This is a standardized three-page form lenders are required to provide within three business days of receiving your application. Use it to compare APR, total loan cost, prepayment penalties, and fees on an apples-to-apples basis.

5. **Ask specifically about rate lock.** Home equity loan rates can change between application and closing. Ask each lender how long they'll hold the quoted rate and whether there's a fee to lock it.

6. **Don't ignore credit unions.** In my experience, credit unions frequently offer lower rates on home equity products than big banks, with more flexible underwriting. The Federal Housing Finance Agency tracks broader mortgage market data, but for home equity products specifically, the best rates often live at institutions you have to seek out rather than ones advertising on every platform.

## When a Home Equity Loan Is the Wrong Tool

I'll be direct: home equity loans are appropriate for specific purposes and genuinely bad choices for others.

Good uses: Home improvements that add value, debt consolidation where you've addressed the spending behavior that created the debt, and major planned expenses with defined costs.

Questionable uses: Consolidating consumer debt without a plan to change what created it. I've watched borrowers pay off $40,000 in credit cards with a home equity loan, then run the cards back up within two years, leaving themselves with both a home equity loan payment and fresh credit card debt.

Bad uses: Covering routine expenses, funding a business with uncertain returns, or investing in volatile assets. Your house is collateral. If the investment doesn't work or the business fails, you can lose your home. No investment return justifies that risk casually.

Also worth considering: if you're in a rising-rate environment and anticipate needing to sell your home within a few years, adding a second mortgage complicates that sale. At closing, both loans get paid off from the proceeds. If values have declined or you haven't built much additional equity, you could end up with less than expected.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How is a home equity loan rate different from a mortgage rate?

Your primary mortgage rate is typically lower because lenders view first-lien loans as less risky. If you default, the first mortgage gets paid before the second. A home equity loan sits in second position, so lenders charge more for that added risk. The premium varies but is commonly 0.5% to 2% above comparable first mortgage rates depending on market conditions and borrower profile.

### Can I negotiate a home equity loan rate?

Yes, more than most people realize. Competing offers are your best leverage. If you bring a Loan Estimate from one lender to another, you create a concrete reference point. Lenders with existing relationships, like your current bank or a credit union you belong to, sometimes have more pricing flexibility than their published rates suggest. Autopay discounts and relationship discounts are often available but rarely volunteered without asking.

### Does my home equity loan rate change after closing?

No. That's the core feature of a fixed-rate home equity loan. Your rate and payment are set at closing and don't change for the life of the loan. This distinguishes it from a HELOC, where the rate floats. Confirm your loan is fixed rate in the Loan Estimate and closing documents before signing.

### What credit score do I need to qualify for a competitive rate?

Most lenders require a minimum score of 620 to 640 to approve a home equity loan, but you won't see competitive pricing until you're at 700 or above. The best rates typically go to borrowers at 760 or higher, with strong equity and low CLTV. If your score is below 700, it may be worth spending a few months improving it before applying. The rate difference on a five-figure loan over 10 years can be substantial.

### Are home equity loan interest payments tax-deductible?

Sometimes, but not always. Under current tax law, interest on a home equity loan is deductible only if the loan proceeds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home securing the loan. Using the money for debt consolidation, a car, or college tuition doesn't qualify. Tax law changes, and your individual situation affects the calculation. Confirm the deductibility with a tax professional before factoring it into your decision.
