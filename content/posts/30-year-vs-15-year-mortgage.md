Borrowers who choose a 30-[year mortgage](/15-year-vs-30-year-mortgage-pros-and-cons/) over a 15-year will pay, on average, more than double the total interest over the life of the loan. Not 50% more. Not "significantly more." More than double. That number stopped a lot of people cold when I used to show it to them across my desk, and honestly, it should.

But here's what I also tell people: that statistic alone will send you in the wrong direction if you let it make the decision for you. I've watched buyers stretch into 15-year payments they couldn't really afford, and I've watched them end up miserable, house-rich and cash-poor, terrified every month that one bad week at work would cost them their home. The math on interest savings is real. The math on your actual life has to come first.

So let's figure out which one is actually right for you, because the answer is genuinely different depending on your situation.

## What the Numbers Actually Look Like

Let me put a real scenario on the table so we're not talking in abstractions.

Say you're borrowing $350,000. As of July 2026, a competitive 30-[year fixed](/current-30-year-fixed-mortgage-rates-forecast-2026/) rate sits somewhere around 6.75%, and a 15-year fixed is closer to 6.0% (rates shift daily, so treat these as illustrative, not a quote). Here's what those two loans look like side by side:

| | 30-Year Fixed | 15-Year Fixed |
|---|---|---|
| Loan amount | $350,000 | $350,000 |
| Interest rate | 6.75% | 6.00% |
| Monthly payment (principal + interest) | ~$2,270 | ~$2,954 |
| Total paid over loan life | ~$817,200 | ~$531,700 |
| Total interest paid | ~$467,200 | ~$181,700 |
| Difference in monthly payment | +$684/mo for 15-yr | |
| Interest savings with 15-year | ~$285,500 | |

That $285,500 in interest savings is genuinely staggering. But the $684 monthly difference is also genuinely real. Over a year, that's $8,208 in extra cash that the 30-year borrower keeps in their pocket each month, money they could put toward retirement accounts, their kids' education, paying down other higher-interest debt, or simply having a financial cushion when life gets weird.

The rate gap matters too. Freddie Mac's Primary Mortgage Market Survey consistently shows 15-year [fixed rates](/should-you-take-an-arm-right-now-to-beat-high-fixed-rates/) running roughly 0.5 to 0.75 percentage points below 30-year rates. That's not nothing. You're getting a lower rate AND paying it off in half the time, which is why the interest savings compound so dramatically.

## The Case for the 15-Year (And It's Stronger Than You Think)

> **Helpful resource:** [The Millionaire Real Estate Investor by Gary Keller](https://www.amazon.com/dp/1492368423?tag=contentportfo-20) is a top-rated option for this. *(As an Amazon Associate this site earns from qualifying purchases.)*



I'll be honest: if you can comfortably afford the higher payment without straining, the 15-year is almost always the mathematically superior choice. Full stop.

You build equity faster. You pay a lower rate. You're done in half the time. For a lot of people, especially those in their late 30s or 40s who want a paid-off house before retirement, that accelerated timeline has real emotional and financial value that doesn't show up in any spreadsheet.

There's also a discipline argument, and I don't mean that in a judgmental way. A 15-year loan forces the payoff. With a 30-year, the intention to "make extra payments" very often dissolves under the pressure of daily life. A home renovation here, a car repair there, a vacation you needed. I've talked to dozens of people who took a 30-year with plans to pay it off in 20 and ended up paying for all 30 years. The 15-year removes the option to drift.

According to data from the Urban Institute, 15-year mortgages made up roughly 9-10% of all mortgage originations in recent years, meaning the vast majority of buyers do choose the 30-year. But among buyers who are refinancing into a shorter term, the 15-year is popular precisely because those borrowers have already lived through the slow-paydown feeling of a 30-year and want out.

## Why Most People Should Probably Start with a 30-Year

This is the part where I'll lose a few people, because the conventional wisdom you hear from financially savvy friends is "get the 15-year, pay less interest, build wealth faster." And they're not wrong. But I think the advice is incomplete.

Here's what I tell people who are on the fence: housing is not your only financial priority.

If you're carrying credit card debt at 22% interest, your 30-year mortgage at 6.75% is not your most expensive debt. Paying it off faster doesn't fix that. If you're not maxing out your 401(k) match at work, you're leaving guaranteed returns on the table. In 2026, the average employer match is worth thousands of dollars per year in free money that many buyers forfeit because they're stretching into a 15-year payment.

There's also the emergency fund question. One thing I learned doing this work is that first-time buyers consistently underestimate how much stuff breaks. In my first year of homeownership, I replaced a water heater ($1,200), had a plumber out twice, and paid to fix a section of fence I didn't even know was my legal responsibility. If your 15-year payment is tight enough that it wipes out your ability to maintain a 3-6 month cash reserve, you are taking on risk that the interest savings don't justify.

The 30-year's lower payment gives you room. Room to save. Room to invest. Room to handle emergencies without putting them on a credit card. Freddie Mac's home buyer resources actually address this directly: affordability isn't just about qualifying for the loan, it's about sustainable ownership.

A second scenario worth walking through:

Buyer in Phoenix, income of $95,000/year, $12,000 in student loans at 5.5%, no employer 401(k) match yet (new job, vesting period) → Takes 30-year mortgage to keep payment lower → Uses $684/month difference to aggressively pay down student loans in 18 months, then redirects to retirement account → At the end of year 3, she's debt-free except the mortgage, has $24,000 more in retirement savings than she would have otherwise, and can now start making extra mortgage payments at will.

Would the 15-year have saved her more interest? Yes. Would it have left her drowning in competing priorities with no flexibility? Also yes.

## The "Just Make Extra Payments" Strategy

You've probably heard this. Take a 30-year, make extra principal payments, and you can pay it off in 20 years or even 15 years without being locked into the higher required payment.

This is actually a legitimate strategy, and I don't want to dismiss it. The math works. Every extra dollar you send in goes directly to principal, shortening your loan term and reducing total interest. Most lenders (check your specific loan documents, because there are exceptions) don't charge prepayment penalties on conventional loans.

The honest downside: as I mentioned, most people don't follow through consistently. Life happens. The extra payment you planned in January becomes smaller in March and nonexistent by July.

If you are disciplined, genuinely disciplined, this approach gives you the best of both worlds: the flexibility of a 30-year payment obligation with the interest-reduction potential of accelerated payoff. If you're not sure you're that disciplined, the 15-year enforces it for you.

Third example: Couple in Austin, refinancing a $280,000 balance → Chose 30-year at 6.625% instead of 15-year at 5.875% → Committed to paying an extra $500/month toward principal → Projected payoff: 21.5 years instead of 30 → Total interest paid: approximately $243,000 instead of $388,000 (30-year with no extra payments) or $154,000 (15-year). A middle path, and one they could actually sustain.

## One Thing That Changes the Whole Calculation

Your investment returns.

If you can take that $684/month difference between the 15-year and 30-year payments and invest it consistently in a diversified index fund, you might come out ahead of the 15-year borrower on a pure net-worth basis, depending on market returns. At a hypothetical 7% average annual return (roughly the historical average for broad stock market index funds, before inflation), $684/month invested over 30 years grows to roughly $825,000. That's more than the $285,500 in interest saved by the 15-year.

I want to be careful here because I don't want to overstate this. Market returns are not guaranteed. Mortgage interest saved is guaranteed. And this comparison assumes perfect discipline in investing every dollar of the difference every month for 30 years, which is an idealized scenario. The research on whether carrying a mortgage to invest in equities ("arbitrage your mortgage") is actually a good idea is genuinely mixed, and your personal risk tolerance matters a lot.

If you're someone who would actually invest the difference and sleep fine knowing your mortgage isn't paid off, this is a real consideration. If you're someone who would probably spend the difference and feel stressed knowing you have 30 years of payments, the 15-year might deliver better real-world outcomes for you specifically, even if the theoretical numbers favor the 30.
