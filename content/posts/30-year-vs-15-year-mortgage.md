Most borrowers who choose a 30-[year mortgage](/15-year-vs-30-year-mortgage-pros-and-cons/) will pay more in interest than they paid for the house itself.

That's not a typo. On a $400,000 loan at 7% interest, a 30-year mortgage generates roughly $558,000 in total interest over the life of the loan. The house cost $400,000. The interest costs almost $140,000 more than that. I remember the [first time](/best-mortgage-lenders-for-first-time-home-buyers-2026/) I pulled that number for a borrower sitting across from me at my desk. She went quiet for a solid ten seconds. "Nobody told me that," she finally said. Nobody usually does.

That doesn't automatically make the 30-year a bad deal. But it does mean you deserve to understand exactly what you're trading when you pick one term over the other, because the difference between a 15-year and a 30-year mortgage is one of the biggest financial decisions most people will ever make, and most people make it based on the monthly payment alone.

What most people don't realize is that the lower rate on the 15-year isn't a promotion or a gift from the lender. Shorter loans carry less risk for the bank (you're a borrower for half as long), so they price them lower. You can verify this pattern yourself by checking [Freddie Mac's home buyer resources](https://myhome.freddiemac.com/), which track the weekly national average for both products going back decades. The spread fluctuates, but the 15-year is almost always cheaper on rate.

## Why Most People Still Pick the 30-Year (And When That's Actually Right)

Here's where I'm going to push back on the conventional advice, because a lot of personal finance writers act like the 30-year is a trap that only financially unsophisticated people fall into. That's not fair, and it's not accurate.

The 30-year's lower payment isn't just psychological comfort. It's real financial flexibility, and depending on your situation, it might be the smarter choice.

Think about this scenario: A first-time buyer in their early 30s, carrying $41,000 in student loans at 6.5%, is deciding between a 15-year and a 30-year mortgage. The 15-year payment would stretch their budget to the point where they have essentially no liquid savings cushion. I've sat with people in exactly this position. What I've seen happen, more than once, is that the person who chose the lower 30-year payment used the $800/month difference to eliminate their student debt in four years, then redirected that same payment toward extra mortgage principal. They built equity almost as fast as a 15-year borrower would have, without ever feeling underwater on cash flow.

The danger with the 30-year isn't the term itself. It's the lifestyle creep that tends to follow when the payment feels "easy."

The truth is, the 30-year can work brilliantly if you're disciplined about what you do with the difference. The 15-year forces that discipline on you automatically. Which one you need depends heavily on your own psychology and your income stability, neither of which shows up on a rate sheet.

## The Real Cost of a Payment Stretch

I want to walk through a real scenario because abstract percentages don't stick the way specific stories do.

**Scenario 1:** Maria, a nurse in Phoenix, buys a $480,000 home in early 2025, puts 10% down, and takes a $432,000 loan. She chooses a 30-year at 6.9%.

- Monthly P&I: approximately $2,853
- Total interest over 30 years: approximately $595,000

**→ Result:** Comfortable monthly payment. But at the end of 30 years, she will have paid over $1 million total for a home that cost $480,000.

**Scenario 2:** Same Maria, same home, same down payment, but she stretches for the 15-year at 6.3%.

- Monthly P&I: approximately $3,717
- Total interest over 15 years: approximately $236,600

**→ Result:** She pays $864 more per month, but saves roughly $358,000 in interest, and owns the home free and clear by age 57 instead of 72.

That last part matters more than people acknowledge. Carrying a mortgage payment into retirement is a real risk. According to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, about 30% of homeowners aged 65 and older were still carrying mortgage debt as of recent years. Many of them are on fixed incomes. The math gets uncomfortable fast when your $2,800 monthly payment represents 40% of your Social Security income.

**Scenario 3:** David, a freelance graphic designer in Austin, earns well but inconsistently. Some months are $11,000, some are $4,500. He chooses the 30-year specifically because it keeps his required payment low during lean months, and he makes aggressive extra principal payments during good months.

**→ Result:** This is actually a legitimate strategy, and the 30-year is the right call here. Locking yourself into a 15-year payment when your income swings $6,500 a month is a foreclosure risk in a bad stretch. The 30-year gives him a floor he can always meet.

## What Nobody Tells You About Building Equity

I thought for years that picking the 15-year was obviously better purely because of equity speed. Then I started looking at the numbers more carefully, and I had to revise that.

Early in a 30-year mortgage, the vast majority of your payment goes to interest. On that $400,000 loan at 6.85%, your first payment of roughly $2,623 breaks down like this: about $2,283 goes to interest, and only around $340 goes to principal. You're not building equity fast at all. That ratio improves over time, but it takes years to shift meaningfully.

On the 15-year at 6.25%, your first payment of about $3,429 breaks down closer to $2,083 to interest and $1,346 to principal. You're building equity roughly four times faster in that first payment.

This matters because home equity is wealth. It's borrowing power. It's the thing that lets you help your kid with a down payment, survive a medical emergency, or convert to a rental property someday. The 15-year borrower has meaningfully more of it, sooner.

That said, if your alternative to a faster mortgage paydown is investing in a diversified index fund, the math can flip. Historically, the S&P 500 has returned around 10% annually before inflation. If your mortgage rate is 6.25%, the mathematical argument for investing the difference rather than paying down the mortgage faster is real. I don't want to oversell it, because investment returns aren't guaranteed and mortgage savings are, but it's a legitimate reason some financially sophisticated people intentionally choose the 30-year.

## How to Actually Make This Decision

Forget what your real estate agent told you. Forget what your parents did. Here's what I'd look at:

First, run your 30-year payment through a stress test: if your income dropped by 30% tomorrow, could you still make it? If yes, you have options. If the 30-year payment already feels tight, a 15-year is probably not realistic.

Second, be honest about your other debt. If you're carrying high-interest consumer debt (anything above 7% or so), paying that down before accelerating your mortgage is almost always the better mathematical move. A [HUD-approved housing counselor](https://www.hud.gov/i_want_to/talk_to_a_housing_counselor) can actually help you model this for free, and I've seen them catch situations that saved borrowers thousands.

Third, think about career trajectory. A 28-year-old in a stable salaried profession with strong raise history is a different borrower than a 52-year-old within ten years of retirement. The younger borrower has more time to absorb a tight month. The older borrower might want the 15-year specifically to be mortgage-free before retirement.

One practical tip I picked up from years of reviewing loan files: if you're genuinely torn, you can take the 30-year and set up a biweekly payment schedule from day one. By making half your monthly payment every two weeks, you end up making 26 half-payments a year, which equals 13 full payments instead of 12. That one extra payment per year can shave four to six years off a 30-year mortgage depending on your rate. It's not the same as a 15-year, but it's a middle path that's real.
