Most borrowers have never heard of a mortgage recast. Their loan officer never mentioned it. Neither did their real estate agent. And that's a shame, because for the right person in the right situation, it's one of the most underused tools in personal finance.

Here's the short version: a recast lets you make a large lump-sum payment toward your principal, and then asks your lender to recalculate (recast) your remaining monthly payment based on that new, lower balance. Your interest rate stays the same. Your loan term stays the same. You just owe less, so your monthly payment drops. That's it. Simple in theory. Genuinely valuable in practice.

## How a Recast Actually Works

Say you bought a home two years ago. Your original loan was $480,000 at a fixed 6.75% rate over 30 years, putting your principal and interest payment somewhere around $3,113 a month. Now you come into $80,000, maybe from an inheritance, a bonus, a business sale, or the proceeds from selling a previous home that finally closed.

You could put that $80,000 toward your principal without recasting, and it would absolutely save you interest over the life of the loan. But your monthly payment wouldn't change at all. Every extra dollar you paid just shortens the amortization schedule quietly, invisibly, without touching your obligation each month.

A recast changes that. You pay the $80,000 down, your new balance drops to roughly $396,000 (give or take, depending on where you are in amortization), and then your lender re-amortizes the loan over the remaining term. Your new monthly payment reflects that lower balance. Instead of $3,113, you might be paying closer to $2,597 a month. That's real money back in your budget every single month for the rest of the loan.

The fee for this? Most lenders charge between $150 and $500 to process the recast. That's not a typo. You're not refinancing. There's no appraisal, no title search, no closing costs that run 2-3% of your loan balance. You fill out a short form, pay a small administrative fee, make the lump-sum payment, and your servicer recalculates. The [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/) has resources that explain how servicers handle these requests, which can help you know what to ask for and what documentation to expect.

I want to be direct here: a recast is not for everyone. It's particularly useful in two situations.

The first is the "contingent buyer" scenario. You bought your new home before your old one sold, so you carried both mortgages for a few months. Now your old home has closed, and you're sitting on the net proceeds. You don't want to refinance (your current rate might actually be fine, or a refi would reset you back to a 30-year term and cost you thousands in fees). You just want your payment on the new home to reflect that big chunk you're about to put down. Recast is the exact right tool.

The second is the person who receives a meaningful windfall and wants to lower their monthly obligations without locking into a new loan. Retirees going onto fixed income think about this a lot. So do people who are planning to reduce their work hours or take a career pivot.

One thing I tell people who are weighing a recast against extra principal payments without recasting: if your cash flow is tight and a lower required monthly payment would give you real breathing room, recast. If you're comfortable with your current payment and you just want to pay the loan off faster or save on interest, you don't need to recast. Just pay extra.

## What a Recast Can't Do

It won't lower your interest rate. If you're at 7.25% and rates have dropped, recasting doesn't help you there. You'd need to refinance for that.

It also won't work on every loan type. FHA loans and VA loans are generally not eligible for recasting. Conventional loans serviced by most major lenders (loans that conform to [Federal Housing Finance Agency](https://www.fhfa.gov/) guidelines) typically are, but you need to ask your specific servicer because this is not a federally mandated feature. It's a lender option, and not all servicers offer it. Call before you assume. Some servicers also have a minimum lump-sum requirement, often $5,000 to $10,000, before they'll process a recast.

Jumbo loans vary wildly. Some jumbo lenders love recasts. Others won't touch them. It depends entirely on who owns your note.

## The Math You Should Run Before You Decide

If you're comparing a recast to a refinance, the math is pretty clear. Refinancing today typically costs 2-3% of your loan balance in closing costs. On a $400,000 loan, that's $8,000 to $12,000 out of pocket, plus you're usually resetting to a fresh 30-year term, which adds years of interest back onto your balance. A recast at $250 in fees, with no term extension? The comparison isn't even close, assuming your current rate is something you can live with.

Where it gets more nuanced is when you're sitting on a large sum and trying to decide whether to recast, refinance, or just invest the money. I'd be careful about anyone who gives you a pat answer there. Whether it makes more financial sense to pay down your mortgage or invest depends on your rate, your risk tolerance, your tax situation, and how close you are to retirement. Run the numbers both ways, and if they're close, talk to a fee-only financial planner before you commit.
