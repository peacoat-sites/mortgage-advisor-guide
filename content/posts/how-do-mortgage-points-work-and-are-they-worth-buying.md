You're probably sitting with a loan estimate right now, staring at a line item that says something like "Discount Points: 1.000 ($3,200)" and wondering whether that's a gift or a trap. Maybe your loan officer mentioned you could "buy the rate down" and made it sound like an obvious win. Here's what I tell people in that moment: slow down. It's not a scam, but it's not always the deal it looks like either.

Let me walk you through exactly how this works, because the math is simple once you see it laid out, and the decision is almost always more personal than financial.

## What a Mortgage Point Actually Is

One point equals 1% of your loan amount. On a $320,000 mortgage, one point costs $3,200. You pay that at closing, in cash, in exchange for a lower interest rate on your loan. Most lenders offer a reduction somewhere in the range of 0.20% to 0.25% per point, though that spread can vary. Don't let anyone tell you there's a universal rate drop per point. There isn't. It depends on the lender, the loan product, the day's pricing, and sometimes frankly what margin the lender is working with.

What you're doing, stripped of any marketing language, is pre-paying interest. You're giving the lender money now so they charge you less every month for the life of the loan. That's the whole mechanism.

There are also "origination points," which are a fee the lender charges for making the loan, not for buying down your rate. These are a cost, full stop. Not the same thing. Make sure you know which kind is on your loan estimate before you decide anything.

## The Breakeven Calculation (Do This Before You Decide Anything)

This is the only math that actually matters.

Take the cost of the point, divide it by your monthly savings. That gives you your breakeven in months. If you're still in the house and carrying that loan past that number, the point paid off. If you sell, refinance, or pay the loan off before then, you lost money.

Here's a concrete example: You're borrowing $400,000. One point costs $4,000. Buying it drops your rate from 7.00% to 6.75%. At 7.00%, your principal and interest payment is roughly $2,661. At 6.75%, it's about $2,594. That's $67 per month in savings.

$4,000 divided by $67 = 59.7 months. Just under five years to break even.

If you know you're moving in three years? Don't buy the point. If you're planting roots for the next decade and you don't expect rates to drop enough to make refinancing worthwhile in the near term? That point might actually earn its keep.

What kills me is how rarely loan officers walk borrowers through this calculation explicitly. Some do. Many don't. The [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's owning-a-home tools](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/) include a loan comparison worksheet that helps you run exactly this kind of side-by-side, and I'd encourage you to use it before your next conversation with your lender.

## The Refinance Problem Nobody Mentions

Here's what the "buy points now" pitch usually skips: if rates drop meaningfully and you refinance, those points are gone.

You paid $4,000 to lower your rate. You enjoyed 28 months of $67 savings ($1,876). Then rates dropped 1.5% and you refinanced into a new loan. You didn't just fail to break even, you paid $4,000 and only recovered less than half of it.

This scenario isn't hypothetical. I watched it play out constantly after 2008 and again in 2020 when rates cratered. Borrowers who had loaded up on points in the years prior found themselves refinancing and essentially donating that cash to their original lender. It's not a catastrophe, but it stings, and nobody needs that regret.

The real question is: what do you think rates are going to do? And look, nobody knows. Not me, not your loan officer, not the economist on the cable news chyron. If you're buying in a high-rate environment and there's genuine reason to think rates may fall within a few years, buying points to lock in your current rate is a riskier bet than it sounds.

## When Points Actually Make Sense

I'm not here to talk you out of points categorically. There are real situations where buying them is the right call.

If you plan to stay in the home for a long time, you're not anticipating a major life change that would trigger a sale or refinance, and you have the cash to buy the points without straining your reserves, then the math can genuinely work in your favor. The savings compound over time. On a 30-year loan you carry for 25 years, that monthly $67 becomes $20,100 in total savings on a $4,000 investment. That's a reasonable return.

Points also make sense when the seller is paying for them. Seller-paid closing costs, sometimes called a seller concession, can include discount points. If a seller agrees to cover $8,000 of your closing costs and you use that toward two points, you're getting rate reduction for free. That changes the calculus entirely. [Freddie Mac's homebuyer resources](https://myhome.freddiemac.com/) have a good breakdown of how seller concessions work within conforming loan guidelines, which is worth reviewing if you're in active negotiations.

One more scenario: if you're on a very tight monthly budget and the lower payment genuinely makes the difference between qualifying comfortably or barely qualifying, paying points to hit a lower rate threshold can be a practical move. Just go in knowing you're optimizing for cash flow, not for long-term return.

## Fractional Points and Negative Points (Yes, Those Exist)

You don't have to buy a full point. Many lenders let you buy 0.5 points or even 0.25 points. Same proportional cost, same proportional rate reduction. Worth asking about if you want to fine-tune your payment.

Negative points, or lender credits, work in reverse: the lender gives you cash toward closing costs in exchange for a higher rate. If you're cash-strapped at closing, this can be genuinely useful. You take a slightly higher rate and the lender covers some of your fees. The tradeoff is a higher monthly payment for the life of the loan, so the breakeven math applies here too, just flipped.

I've seen buyers use lender credits strategically to reduce what they need to bring to the table at closing, especially in high-cost markets where down payment, closing costs, and reserves are all competing for the same pool of money. It's not a bad tool. It just needs to be a conscious choice, not something that happened to you.

## The Tax Angle (Briefly, Because It Matters)

Points paid on a purchase loan are generally deductible in the year you pay them if you itemize. That's a real benefit. On a refinance, the rules are different: you typically have to deduct them ratably over the life of the loan rather than all at once. This isn't advice, check with your tax professional, the rules have nuances depending on your situation. But if you are an itemizer and you're buying a primary residence, the deductibility of points can meaningfully improve your effective return on them.

Most people don't itemize anymore since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act roughly doubled the standard deduction. So for many borrowers, the tax benefit doesn't move the needle. But for high earners with large mortgages and other deductions stacking up, it can.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many points can you buy on a mortgage?

Most lenders cap discount points somewhere between 3 and 4 points, though the ceiling varies by loan type and lender guidelines. Buying more than 2 points is rare and usually only makes financial sense in very specific circumstances. Ask your lender what their maximum is and what rate reduction each additional point actually buys, since the reduction per point often diminishes after the first one.

### Do mortgage points expire or disappear if you refinance?

Yes. When you refinance, you're taking out a new loan, and any points you paid on the original loan don't carry over. Whatever you paid is gone, and you start from scratch with the new loan's terms. This is why your expected time in the loan is the single most important factor in deciding whether to buy points.

### Can you roll points into the loan instead of paying cash at closing?

Generally no, not with conventional or government-backed loans. Points are a closing cost, and while some closing costs can be rolled into certain loan types under certain conditions, discount points specifically are intended to be paid upfront. If a lender is offering to "roll your points in," read that contract carefully. You may actually be looking at a higher loan balance with a marginally better rate, which needs its own analysis.

### Are discount points the same as an origination fee?

No, and this confusion trips people up all the time. Origination points (or origination fees) are what the lender charges for processing and making the loan. You pay them regardless of what rate you get. Discount points are optional and specifically purchase a rate reduction. Both appear on your Loan Estimate, sometimes on the same line, which is why you should ask your lender to break them apart explicitly.

### Is buying points worth it in a high interest rate environment?

It depends entirely on your breakeven timeline and your refinance expectations. In a high-rate environment where many buyers expect rates to fall within a few years, buying points carries more risk because a refinance would wipe out the benefit before you've recovered the cost. That doesn't make it automatically wrong, but it does mean you should stress-test the decision against a scenario where you refinance in 24 to 36 months. If the answer still works, go for it.
