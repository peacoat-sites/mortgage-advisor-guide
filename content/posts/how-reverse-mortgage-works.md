Sixty-two years old, equity-rich, and cash-poor. That's the situation I watched play out dozens of times in my underwriting career, and it's exactly the person reverse mortgages were designed for. The house is worth $480,000. The mortgage is paid off. But Social Security barely covers the electric bill, and the savings account is thinner than it should be.

A [reverse mortgage](/is-a-reverse-mortgage-a-good-idea-for-retirees/) sounds, on paper, like the answer. And sometimes it genuinely is. But I've seen borrowers sign closing documents without truly understanding what they'd agreed to, and I've seen adult children blindsided when they inherited a house underwater with a loan balance they didn't know existed. So let me walk you through how these things actually work, what they cost (more than you think), and when they make sense versus when you should run.

The core idea is straightforward: instead of you paying the bank every month, the bank pays you. You're borrowing against the equity in your home, and the loan doesn't come due until you sell, move out permanently, or die. No monthly payments required. Sounds great. The catch, and there are several, is that interest keeps accruing on the balance the whole time, and that balance can grow a lot faster than most people expect.

## What You're Actually Borrowing

The government-backed version is called a [Home Equity](/home-equity-loan-rates/) Conversion Mortgage, or HECM (pronounced "heck-em"). It's insured by FHA and represents the vast majority of reverse mortgages issued in the U.S. today. There are also proprietary reverse mortgages from private lenders, which sometimes allow higher loan amounts on higher-value homes, but they don't carry the same federal protections, so I'd look at those carefully.

With a HECM, the amount you can borrow depends on three things: your age (or the age of the younger spouse, if there are two borrowers), current interest rates, and the appraised value of your home capped at the FHA lending limit, which as of July 2026 is $1,209,750. Generally, the older you are and the lower the rates, the more you can access. A 75-year-old homeowner will typically qualify for a higher percentage of their home's value than a 62-year-old.

You don't have to take the money as a lump sum. You can choose a line of credit (which actually grows over time, a feature most people don't realize exists), a monthly payment to yourself, or some combination. The line of credit option is often the most flexible and, honestly, probably underused. If you don't need cash [right now](/should-you-take-an-arm-right-now-to-beat-high-fixed-rates/) but want a safety net, a growing line of credit sitting there untouched can be surprisingly powerful 10 years down the road.

## The Real Costs (Which Are Not Small)

Here's where I have to be blunt, because the marketing for reverse mortgages tends to gloss over this part.

Upfront costs on a HECM are real money. The mandatory mortgage insurance premium alone is 2% of the appraised value (up to the lending limit) at closing. On a $400,000 home, that's $8,000, paid at closing, typically rolled into the loan. Then there's an origination fee: lenders can charge 2% of the first $200,000 of home value and 1% after that, with a cap of $6,000. Add in the appraisal ($400–$700), title insurance, closing costs, and a required counseling session (more on that in a minute), and you're often looking at $12,000 to $18,000 in upfront costs on a mid-value home.

After closing, there's an ongoing annual mortgage insurance premium of 0.5% of the loan balance. That compounds. If you live in the house for 20 years, the total interest and insurance costs can eat through a significant chunk of your equity. I've run scenarios where a borrower who took $150,000 at age 68 ended up with a loan balance well north of $300,000 by their late 80s, depending on the rate environment. That's not unusual. It's math, not predatory lending, but borrowers deserve to see those projections before they sign.

What you still have to pay even after you get a reverse mortgage: property taxes, homeowner's insurance, and basic home maintenance. This trips people up. If you fall behind on taxes or insurance, the lender can call the loan due. That's happened to a lot of borrowers who were cash-strapped to begin with and treated the reverse mortgage as a fix-all.

## The Counseling Requirement (Don't Skip This)

Before you can even apply for a HECM, you're required by law to complete a counseling session with a HUD-approved counselor. I know it sounds like a bureaucratic checkbox, but I'd encourage you to treat it as the most useful hour you'll spend in this whole process. [HUD-approved housing counselors](https://www.hud.gov/i_want_to/talk_to_a_housing_counselor) will walk through your specific numbers, explain alternatives you may not have considered, and they're not paid by the lender, which matters.

The fee is usually $125 to $200, though it can be waived if you can't afford it. Take the session seriously. Come with questions. Bring an adult child or trusted friend if you can. I've seen borrowers breeze through it just to get the certificate, then regret not paying attention when something unexpected happened two years later.

## How Repayment Actually Works

The loan doesn't come due while you're living in the home as your primary residence. If you move into a nursing facility for more than 12 consecutive months, the loan becomes due. If you sell, it's due. If you die, your heirs typically have 6 months (sometimes extended to 12) to settle up.

Here's something that surprises a lot of heirs: they're never personally responsible for more than the home is worth. Because HECMs are non-recourse loans, if the loan balance exceeds the home's value, FHA's insurance covers the difference. Your heirs don't owe the gap out of pocket. That's the protection the ongoing mortgage insurance premium is actually paying for.

If your heirs want to keep the home, they have to pay off the loan balance, or 95% of the current appraised value, whichever is less. In practice, that usually means getting a traditional mortgage or paying cash. Some families plan for this. Many don't, and that's where things get messy.

A worked scenario to make this concrete:

Maria, age 70, has a home worth $350,000 free and clear. She takes a HECM line of credit, qualifying for roughly $196,000 based on her age and current rates. She draws $2,000/month for 10 years. At a 6.5% interest rate environment (hypothetical, not a guarantee), her loan balance after a decade could be approximately $330,000 to $360,000, potentially exceeding her original home value depending on appreciation. Her daughter inherits the home, owes no more than 95% of the then-appraised value, and has 6 months to refinance or sell. If the house appreciated to $500,000, the daughter pays off the loan and keeps $140,000+ in equity. If the house barely moved, the math gets tighter.

That's a real range of outcomes, and both are possible. Which outcome you're headed toward matters enormously.

## When a Reverse Mortgage Actually Makes Sense

I've seen them work well in pretty specific situations. A borrower who wants to delay tapping Social Security (to get a higher monthly benefit later) and uses the reverse mortgage line of credit to bridge the gap for a few years. A couple where one spouse has high medical costs and they need cash flow without selling. Someone who truly plans to stay in the home for life and whose heirs have no interest in inheriting the property.

What I'd push back on is using a reverse mortgage to fund discretionary spending, like travel or home renovations that aren't medically necessary, without being clear-eyed about the long-term balance growth. The loan grows whether you're using it actively or not, once you have a balance.

[Freddie Mac's home buyer resources](https://myhome.freddiemac.com/) include some useful tools for thinking about long-term home equity and retirement planning that can give you a broader frame before you commit to any single product.

For someone considering this, the best move I can suggest is getting projections from the counselor showing what your loan balance might look like at 5, 10, 15, and 20 years at different interest rate assumptions. Most people don't ask for these. Ask for them.

One more scenario worth spelling out:

James, age 63 and recently widowed, has a $280,000 home and wants to stay there but can't afford the upkeep on a fixed income. He takes a HECM for $112,000, draws a $900/month tenure payment for as long as he lives in the home. If he passes at age 82 (19 years later), his estate faces a loan balance likely in the $280,000–$320,000 range. His son, who doesn't want the home, lists it, nets $400,000 at sale, pays off the balance, and keeps the remainder. James lived in his home, maintained his dignity, and his son came out fine. That's the best-case version of this product actually doing its job.
