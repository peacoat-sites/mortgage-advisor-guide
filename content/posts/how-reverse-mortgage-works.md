Most people assume a [reverse mortgage](/is-a-reverse-mortgage-a-good-idea-for-retirees/) is some kind of last resort ,  something desperate people use when they've completely run out of options. I thought the same thing for most of my career as an underwriter. Then I spent a few months reviewing files for a lender that specialized in them, and what I found genuinely surprised me. Not because they're secretly great. They're not, for most people. But because the real story is a lot more complicated than "predatory scam" OR "brilliant retirement tool," which is the way most of the internet wants to frame it.

So let me tell you what I actually learned from sitting inside that process.

A reverse mortgage is a loan against your home where, instead of you paying the lender each month, the lender pays you (or hands you a lump sum, or opens a credit line). You don't make monthly payments. The balance grows over time, and the whole thing gets settled when you sell the house, move out permanently, or die. That's the core mechanic. Everything complicated about reverse mortgages flows from those three facts.

## The Basic Mechanics (and Where Things Get Slippery)

Here's the version nobody explains clearly: a reverse mortgage doesn't eliminate your financial responsibilities. You still own the home. You still owe property taxes. You're still on the hook for homeowner's insurance and upkeep. The loan documents are extremely specific about this, and the part that shocked me when I was reading files is how often borrowers genuinely hadn't absorbed it. I saw foreclosure initiations on reverse [mortgage borrowers](/a-fed-rate-hike-is-now-on-the-table-what-mortgage-borrowers-should-do/) who hadn't paid their property taxes. In some counties, that tax bill might be $3,000 a year. Doesn't sound like much until you're 74 and on a fixed income and you thought the whole point of the reverse mortgage was that you'd never owe anything again.

The [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/) has documented this exact problem. Borrowers understand the "no monthly payments" part perfectly. They don't always fully internalize that defaulting on taxes or insurance can trigger a foreclosure just like missing a payment on a regular mortgage.

The loan balance grows because interest accrues every single month. If you borrow $150,000 at 6.5% interest, roughly $812 gets added to what you owe each month, even if you never touch another penny. Compound that over ten or fifteen years and the number climbing against your home's equity starts to feel uncomfortable. That's not a flaw in the product exactly, but it's the thing that blindsides heirs who thought there'd be equity left to inherit.

## Who Actually Qualifies

> **Helpful resource:** [Mortgages for Dummies by Eric Tyson](https://www.amazon.com/dp/1119697026?tag=contentportfo-20) is a top-rated option for this. *(As an Amazon Associate this site earns from qualifying purchases.)*



For the most common type, the [Home Equity](/home-equity-for-renovations/) Conversion Mortgage (HECM), which is federally insured and regulated, you need to be at least 62 years old, own your home outright or have considerable equity, live in it as your primary residence, and complete a counseling session with a HUD-approved counselor. That counseling session is mandatory and it's not a rubber stamp. A good counselor will actually walk through the numbers with you.

There are also proprietary reverse mortgages (sometimes called "jumbo reverse mortgages") for higher-value homes, offered by private lenders outside the federal program. Some allow borrowers as young as 55. These don't have the same federal consumer protections, which matters.

The amount you can borrow isn't just your home's value. It depends on your age, the current interest rate, and the appraised value (capped at $1,149,825 for HECMs as of this year per the [Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA)](https://www.fhfa.gov/)). Older borrowers generally get more, because the loan is expected to run for fewer years.

## What It Actually Costs

This is the table I wish someone had handed me when I first started reviewing these files. The upfront costs are genuinely substantial.

| Cost | Typical Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Origination fee | Up to $6,000 | Capped by HUD for HECMs; 2% of first $200k, 1% above |
| Upfront mortgage insurance premium (MIP) | 2% of appraised value or lending limit | So on a $400k home, that's $8,000 upfront |
| Annual MIP | 0.5% of loan balance per year | Compounds into the loan balance |
| Appraisal | $400-$600 typically | Required, paid upfront out of pocket |
| Title insurance, closing costs | $1,000-$3,000+ | Varies significantly by state and loan size |
| HUD counseling | $125-$200 | Some agencies offer it free |

A concrete example of how this plays out: 74-year-old homeowner in suburban Phoenix, home appraised at $520,000, no existing mortgage. She draws a $220,000 lump sum at closing. Before she receives a dollar, roughly $10,400 goes to upfront MIP, up to $6,000 in origination fees, maybe $2,400 in closing costs and appraisal. The loan balance starts around $219,000 (after fees rolled in). At 7% interest, she's adding about $1,277 per month to what she owes. After ten years of no additional draws: the balance has grown to somewhere around $480,000, depending on compounding specifics. If the house has only appreciated modestly, that's eaten most of the equity. Her heirs will find this out at the worst possible time.

I'm not saying this to scare people off. I'm saying it because this math should be part of the decision.

## The Three Ways to Receive the Money

You have options, and they're meaningfully different:

**Lump sum** gives you all the money at once at a fixed interest rate. This is the only HECM option that comes with a fixed rate. It tends to be the most expensive in long-term interest charges and it's the option most associated with bad outcomes, because a large pile of cash is a target for family members, scammers, and impulse decisions.

**Monthly payments** come in two forms: "tenure" (you get a set amount every month for as long as you live in the home) or "term" (payments for a fixed number of years you choose). Tenure payments are essentially an annuity-like stream and, honestly, this is the structure that makes the most logical sense to me for most borrowers who need regular income.

**Line of credit** is the one financial planners most often mention when they say reverse mortgages can be used "strategically." You draw only what you need, when you need it, and here's the part that surprises most people: the unused portion of the credit line actually grows over time at the same rate as the interest being charged on the loan. So the longer you don't touch it, the more you can eventually draw. This is a legitimate feature, not a gimmick, and it's the mechanism behind some of the more sophisticated retirement income strategies.

You can also combine approaches, for example, a monthly payment plus a smaller line of credit as a buffer.

## When It Makes Sense (Honestly)

I'll be honest: I came out of my time reviewing those files more skeptical than when I went in. For most people under 70 with meaningful equity, other options deserve a real look first: a cash-out refinance, a HELOC, downsizing to unlock equity, or a bridge loan to cover a specific gap. Those tools don't eat into equity the same way and they don't carry $10,000+ in upfront costs.

Where I've genuinely changed my thinking is for a narrower group:

Someone who is 75+, living in a paid-off home, whose only significant asset is that home, and who needs income to cover living costs or healthcare. For them, the "no monthly payment" structure isn't just a marketing line. It's the difference between staying in the home and not staying in the home.

The line of credit strategy for someone who has other retirement income but wants a growing backup reserve also has real merit. The research here is mixed on how universally applicable this is, but academics like Wade Pfau have published reasonably solid work on using a reverse mortgage credit line as a sequence-of-returns buffer in retirement portfolios. Worth reading before dismissing.

What surprised me was the number of borrowers in those files who had heirs who were completely unprepared. The heirs have options when the borrower dies (sell the home, pay off the loan balance, or refinance), but they typically have only 30 days to notify the servicer and about 6 months total to settle things. That timeline can be brutal if the estate is complicated or if the heirs are in denial about what's happening.
