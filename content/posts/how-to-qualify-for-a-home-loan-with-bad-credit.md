Most people assume that a credit score below 620 is basically a closed door. You apply, you get declined, you wait years to try again. I believed that too, early in my career. Then I spent sixteen years watching people with scores in the 500s close on homes while borrowers with 680s got turned away. The score is one piece. The full picture is a lot more complicated, and a lot more hopeful, than most loan officers will take the time to explain.

## What "Bad Credit" Actually Means to a Lender

Here's what surprises most borrowers: there's no single universal definition of bad credit in mortgage lending. A 580 score might qualify you for an FHA loan with one lender and get you declined at another. A 620 might work for a conventional loan through one investor and fall short at a different one.

The number on your credit report is a starting point, not a verdict.

What lenders are actually trying to answer is a question: given everything I know about this borrower, how likely are they to make their payments? The score summarizes a lot of that. But it doesn't tell the whole story. Your income stability, your assets, your employment history, the size of your down payment, your debt-to-income ratio, and even the type of property you're buying all feed into that answer.

I've seen underwriters approve loans for borrowers with 560 scores who had two years of steady income, zero late payments in the past 12 months, and 20% down. I've also seen 650 score borrowers get declined because they had four open collections, a recent 90-day late payment, and virtually no assets in reserve. The score is a summary, not a sentence.

That said, scores below 580 do close off most conventional lending options. Let's talk about what's actually available.

This is where most borrowers don't dig deep enough, because most loan officers only bring up one or two options. There are actually several distinct paths worth understanding.

**FHA Loans** are the most well-known route for credit-challenged borrowers, and for good reason. The Federal Housing Administration backs these loans, which lets lenders take on more risk than they'd accept with a conventional loan. The minimum credit score for an FHA loan is technically 500, but there's a catch most people don't hear: borrowers with scores between 500 and 579 must put down at least 10%. Drop to a 580 and that minimum down payment falls to 3.5%. Most lenders also add their own "lender overlays," meaning their internal minimum might be 580 or 620 even though FHA technically allows 500. You have to shop.

**VA Loans** don't have an official credit score minimum set by the Department of Veterans Affairs. The VA doesn't actually make the loans; approved lenders do. Those lenders typically set their own minimums, often around 580 to 620. But VA loans also have favorable underwriting guidelines overall, and a strong residual income can compensate for credit weakness. If you're a veteran or active-duty service member, this is your first call to make.

**USDA Loans** are geographic and income-restricted, but if you qualify, they're powerful. They offer 100% financing (no down payment required) and relatively flexible credit standards. Most USDA lenders want to see at least a 640 score, though some approved lenders will manually underwrite applications with lower scores.

**Non-QM Loans** are outside the "Qualified Mortgage" guidelines that most lenders follow. These products can accommodate credit scores as low as 500 in some cases, but you'll pay for the flexibility, often in significantly higher interest rates and fees. I've seen clients use non-QM loans to get into a home, then refinance once they'd rebuilt their credit over two or three years. It's a strategy, not a forever product.

## The Variables Lenders Weight Alongside Your Score

The research here is genuinely mixed on which compensating factors matter most, and the answer varies by loan type and lender. But after years of reading guidelines and watching underwriting decisions, a few things consistently move the needle.

**Debt-to-income ratio (DTI)** might matter as much as your score. DTI is the percentage of your gross monthly income that goes toward debt payments, including the proposed mortgage. FHA generally allows up to 43% DTI, and sometimes higher with strong compensating factors. If your score is low but your income is solid and your existing debts are minimal, that combination can carry significant weight. I've seen underwriters get creative for a borrower at 580 with a 32% DTI.

**Payment history in the last 12 to 24 months** is something underwriters look at separately from the summary score. A bankruptcy five years ago looks different from a 90-day late payment six months ago. Demonstrating that whatever happened is behind you, and that you've been consistently on time since, matters more than people realize.

**Down payment size** beyond the minimum is a real lever. A larger down payment reduces the lender's risk and can sometimes unlock approvals that wouldn't happen at the minimum. It also reduces your loan-to-value ratio, which affects your mortgage insurance cost on FHA loans.

**Cash reserves** are often overlooked by first-time buyers. Lenders like to see that you'll have money left over after closing. Even one or two months of mortgage payments sitting in a savings account signals stability.

**Length and stability of employment** carries weight, especially if you've been in the same field for several years. A two-year employment gap is a problem. A one-month gap because you changed jobs in the same industry often isn't.

## A Step-by-Step Plan to Strengthen Your Application Before You Apply

Don't just apply and hope. Work the variables first.

**Step 1: Get your actual scores.** All three bureaus. Not the "educational" scores from free apps. Pull your official FICO scores, because those are what mortgage lenders use. AnnualCreditReport.com is the legitimate source for free reports. The scores themselves usually cost a small fee, but they're worth having.

**Step 2: Dispute errors.** About 25% of credit reports contain errors significant enough to affect a score, according to Federal Trade Commission research. Medical collections with wrong amounts, accounts that aren't yours, duplicate derogatory items. Dispute them. The process is free and documented in the Fair Credit Reporting Act. A single successful dispute can move your score meaningfully.

**Step 3: Reduce credit card utilization.** This is the fastest lever most people have. If you're carrying balances close to your credit limits, paying those down before you apply can raise your score within one to two billing cycles. The target is below 30% utilization per card. Below 10% is better.

**Step 4: Stop applying for new credit.** Every hard inquiry dings your score a few points. Avoid opening new cards, financing furniture, or taking out any new installment loan in the 6 to 12 months before you plan to apply for a mortgage.

**Step 5: Document 12 months of on-time payments.** Make absolutely everything on time, every single month. Utilities, rent, phone bill, car payment, existing credit cards. Some loan programs can even use rent payment history as a positive factor if you can document it.

**Step 6: Talk to a HUD-approved housing counselor before you apply.** This is free, and these counselors have seen thousands of situations like yours. The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development maintains a directory of approved agencies. They'll give you a realistic picture of where you stand and what to fix. Freddie Mac's home buyer resources at [myhome.freddiemac.com](https://myhome.freddiemac.com/) also offer tools and educational material that are genuinely useful for this process.

**Step 7: Shop multiple lenders.** I cannot stress this enough. Lender overlays mean that different lenders will have different minimums for the same loan program. Getting declined at one lender does not mean the answer is no. It means you haven't found the right lender yet. Multiple mortgage inquiries within a 45-day window are typically treated as a single inquiry for scoring purposes under most FICO models.

## What to Expect: The Honest Version

I'll be honest: qualifying for a mortgage with bad credit usually costs more money. That's the trade-off, and you should go in with clear eyes.

FHA loans require both an upfront mortgage insurance premium (currently 1.75% of the loan amount, rolled into the loan) and an annual mortgage insurance premium paid monthly. That annual MIP doesn't automatically drop off the way private mortgage insurance does on conventional loans. For most FHA borrowers who put down less than 10%, you'll pay MIP for the life of the loan unless you refinance into a conventional loan later.

Higher interest rates are also likely if your score is below 640. The FHFA's loan-level price adjustments mean that lenders charge more for riskier loans. You may not see these costs as a separate line item, but they're built into your rate. A borrower at 750 and a borrower at 580 might be offered very different rates on the same loan product.

None of this means don't do it. What surprised me early in my career was how many clients used a more expensive loan to get into a home, built equity, cleaned up their credit, and refinanced into much better terms within three to four years. The house appreciated, the credit improved, and the strategy worked. But it requires a plan, not just a prayer.
