You've got $80,000 in home equity and a major expense coming up. Maybe it's a kitchen remodel, a kid heading to college, or high-interest credit card debt you want to consolidate. You talk to your loan officer, and they mention two options almost in the same breath: a HELOC or a home equity loan. They make it sound interchangeable. It isn't. The difference between these two products can cost you thousands of dollars or, in some cases, save you thousands, depending entirely on how your situation actually unfolds. After 16 years of watching borrowers sign documents they didn't fully understand, I want to give you the real breakdown.

## What You're Actually Borrowing Against

Both products use your home as collateral. That part everyone understands. What gets murkier is how the equity calculation works and why it matters more than most borrowers realize.

Lenders don't let you borrow against 100% of your equity. Most cap the total loan-to-value ratio, meaning all debt secured by the home, at somewhere between 80% and 90%. So if your home is worth $400,000 and you have a $280,000 first mortgage, your total debt can't exceed roughly $320,000 to $360,000. That leaves you with $40,000 to $80,000 in accessible equity, not the full $120,000 you might have calculated in your head.

This is the number that should anchor every conversation you have with a lender. The [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/) has solid resources on how combined loan-to-value ratios work, and I'd encourage you to read them before you sit across from anyone trying to sell you a product.

One more thing on collateral: because your home is on the line with both products, default consequences are serious. I've seen clients treat HELOCs like a credit card with a generous limit. It's not. Missed payments on either product can lead to foreclosure. That reality deserves to sit with you before you move forward.

## HELOC: The Flexible Option With a Catch

A HELOC, or home equity line of credit, works more like a credit card than a loan. You're approved for a maximum credit limit, and you draw from it as needed during what's called the draw period, typically 10 years. You only pay interest on what you actually use. Then the repayment period kicks in, usually another 10 to 20 years, and you pay down principal plus interest.

The flexibility is genuinely appealing. If you're remodeling a bathroom and you're not sure whether the contractor's bid will come in at $18,000 or $30,000, a HELOC lets you draw what you need when you need it. You're not sitting on a lump sum of borrowed money accumulating interest while you wait for the tile to be delivered.

But here's the catch, and it's the one loan officers tend to downplay: HELOCs almost always carry variable interest rates. Your initial rate might look attractive. What matters is what it looks like 18 months into your draw period when rates have moved. Most HELOCs are tied to the prime rate, which itself tracks closely to the federal funds rate. When the Fed moves, your payment moves.

I'll be honest: the interest rate environment of the last few years has made this conversation much more important than it was when rates sat near zero. Borrowers who opened HELOCs expecting stable payments got a rude lesson in index rate mechanics.

Some lenders do offer rate caps, both periodic caps (limiting how much the rate can rise in a single adjustment period) and lifetime caps (the maximum it can ever reach). Always ask for both numbers in writing. If the loan officer can't tell you what your lifetime cap is, that's a problem.

There's also the structure of payments during the draw period to watch. Some HELOCs allow interest-only payments during the draw period, which sounds great until you realize you haven't reduced your principal at all, and now the repayment period hits and your payment jumps significantly. This payment shock is real and predictable, yet I've watched countless borrowers walk into it with no idea it was coming.

## Home Equity Loan: Predictability at a Price

A home equity loan gives you a lump sum upfront, a fixed interest rate, and a fixed monthly payment over the loan term, typically 5 to 30 years. You know exactly what you're paying on day one. You know what you'll pay on day 3,600. That clarity has real value.

This is the right product if you have a defined, one-time need. You're paying off $55,000 in debt. You're putting a new roof on the house with a firm contractor quote. You need exactly $40,000 for a specific purpose and you're not coming back for more. In those cases, the lump-sum structure isn't a limitation. It's a feature.

What surprised me when I dug deeper into borrower outcomes is how often people choose a HELOC for a defined expense simply because the loan officer framed it as "more flexible." Flexibility you don't need isn't a benefit. It's just an open line of credit attached to your house.

The downside of a home equity loan is that you're paying interest on the full amount from day one, even if you haven't spent all of it yet. If your project runs under budget or gets delayed, you're still on the hook for the full loan balance. That can sting.

Closing costs are also a factor. Home equity loans typically carry closing costs similar to a first mortgage, often 2% to 5% of the loan amount. HELOCs may have lower upfront costs, though some lenders charge appraisal fees, origination fees, and annual maintenance fees. Always ask for the loan estimate documents for both products side by side.

## Side-by-Side Comparison: Which Fits Your Situation

Here's a clean comparison to cut through the noise:

| Feature | HELOC | Home Equity Loan |
|---|---|---|
| Disbursement | Draw as needed (up to limit) | Lump sum at closing |
| Interest rate | Variable (usually prime-based) | Fixed |
| Monthly payment | Varies with usage and rate | Fixed and predictable |
| Best for | Ongoing or uncertain expenses | One-time, defined expense |
| Payment during draw | Often interest-only option | Full principal + interest |
| Risk if rates rise | Payment increases | No impact |
| Closing costs | Often lower, but fees vary | Typically 2% to 5% |
| Access to funds | Revolving credit line | One-time disbursement |
| Foreclosure risk | Yes | Yes |

This table won't make the decision for you. But it should make the trade-offs clearer.

## How to Actually Evaluate Your Options: A Practical Process

Before you call a lender, do this work yourself. It takes maybe an hour and it changes every conversation you'll have afterward.

**Step 1: Define the expense precisely.** Write down what you're actually spending the money on and whether the amount is fixed or variable. A kitchen remodel with multiple contractors and an evolving scope is different from a single medical bill.

**Step 2: Calculate your accessible equity.** Get a reasonable estimate of your home's current market value, subtract your mortgage balance, then apply an 80% to 85% combined loan-to-value threshold. That's your realistic ceiling, not what an optimistic lender might offer.

**Step 3: Run a payment scenario on both products.** For the home equity loan, use a standard amortization calculator with your estimated loan amount and rate. For the HELOC, run the same numbers, then run them again assuming your rate increases by 2 percentage points. Can you absorb that payment? [Freddie Mac's home buyer resources](https://myhome.freddiemac.com/) include financial planning tools that can help you stress-test scenarios like this.

**Step 4: Ask every lender the same questions.** What's the index? What's the margin? What are the periodic and lifetime rate caps? What are the total closing costs? Are there annual fees? Prepayment penalties? Get answers in writing. If a lender resists putting specifics on paper, walk.

**Step 5: Model the total cost over time.** A lower initial rate on a HELOC doesn't mean it's cheaper. Calculate total interest paid over the expected life of the loan under different rate scenarios. The math sometimes surprises people.

## Tax Deductibility: The Fine Print That Matters

This is an area where a lot of outdated advice still circulates. Since the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, the interest on home equity loans and HELOCs is only deductible if the funds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home that secures the loan. Using a HELOC to consolidate credit card debt or pay a tuition bill? That interest is not deductible.

The research here is genuinely mixed on how much this affects borrower decisions in practice, but I can tell you anecdotally that plenty of borrowers still assume all home equity interest is deductible. It isn't. Talk to a tax professional about your specific situation before you factor deductibility into your cost comparison.

The honest truth about HELOCs and home equity loans is that neither is universally better. The right answer depends on whether your expense is fixed or variable, how you'd handle rising payments, and how precisely you can define what you're actually spending. The borrowers I've seen make the best decisions are the ones who do the math themselves before they talk to a lender, so the conversation is about confirming their analysis rather than accepting someone else's. Do that work first, and you'll know which product fits before anyone has a chance to steer you toward the one that earns them more.
