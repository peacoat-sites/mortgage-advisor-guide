You probably already know you have equity in your home. What you might not know is exactly how much, or whether the number you're thinking of is accurate. That gap matters more than most people realize, especially if you're considering refinancing, taking out a home equity loan, or just trying to nail down your actual net worth.

Here's how to calculate it, what can throw off your number, and why your lender's figure might differ from what you're expecting.

## The Basic Formula (And Why It's Just a Starting Point)

Home equity is the difference between what your home is currently worth and what you still owe on it. The math itself is straightforward:

**Current Market Value minus Outstanding Mortgage Balance(s) equals Your Equity**

Your home is worth $420,000 today and you owe $275,000 on your mortgage? You have $145,000 in equity.

But here's where most people stumble: they use the wrong number for one side of that equation, or both. The purchase price isn't the market value. The original loan amount isn't what you owe today. You need current, accurate numbers for each, and getting them takes more work than people expect.

## Finding Your Current Mortgage Balance

This side's easier to pin down. Your mortgage servicer, the company you send payments to, can give you your exact payoff amount at any given date. Log into your account online or call them and ask for a "payoff quote." It'll be slightly higher than your account balance because it includes interest accrued through the payoff date plus sometimes a small processing fee, usually a few hundred dollars.

Don't just glance at last month's statement and move on. Mortgage balances change daily because of how interest accrues. If you're doing serious financial planning, get a payoff quote for a specific date.

If you have a second mortgage, a home equity line of credit (HELOC), or any other lien on the property, those balances count too. Add them all together. I've seen people forget about a HELOC they opened years ago and then look genuinely confused when their equity calculation doesn't match what a lender is seeing. Every debt secured by your home reduces your equity.

## The Trickier Side: Figuring Out What Your Home Is Actually Worth

| Valuation Method | Cost | Accuracy | Best For | Key Limitation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Automated Valuation Model (AVM) | Free | Often substantial error (can be $50,000+ off) | Rough ballpark only | Algorithm-based, ignores unique property features |
| Comparative Market Analysis (CMA) | Free (from agent) | More reliable than AVM | Planning to borrow or refinance within 6+ months | Not an official appraisal |
| Certified Appraisal | $400-$700+ | Gold standard | Major financial decisions, refinancing, home equity loans | Most expensive option |

This is where things get genuinely complicated, and where I want you to slow down.

You have several options for estimating your home's current market value, and they're not all equal.

**Automated Valuation Models (AVMs)** are the tools behind Zillow's "Zestimate," Redfin's estimate, and similar features. They pull data from public records and recent sales and spit out a number instantly. They're convenient and free. They're also often wrong. Zillow itself has published its own median error rate, and in markets where homes are less homogeneous or recent sales data is sparse, that error can be substantial. I've seen AVMs come in $50,000 or more off from a certified appraisal in certain neighborhoods. Use them as a rough ballpark, nothing more.

**Comparative Market Analysis (CMA)** is what a real estate agent provides, usually for free if they're hoping to earn your business. A good agent will pull genuinely comparable recent sales in your neighborhood and give you a more thoughtful estimate than any algorithm can. It still isn't an appraisal, but it's a useful data point, especially if the agent has recent experience selling homes nearby.

**A certified appraisal** is the gold standard. A licensed appraiser physically inspects your property, compares it to recent sales, and produces a written report. It typically costs between $400 and $700 for a standard single-family home, sometimes more in high-cost markets. If you're making a major financial decision based on your equity, this is money well spent. Lenders will order their own appraisal anyway when you apply for a [home equity loan or refinance](/home-equity-loan-rates/), but knowing the number ahead of time prevents surprises.

Here's what I tell people who ask which method to use. If you're just curious, an AVM is fine. If you're planning to [borrow against your equity](/home-equity-for-renovations/) or refinance in the next six months, spend the money on a real appraisal or at least get a CMA from two different agents and see where they land.

## A Worked Example, Step by Step

You bought your home in 2018 for $330,000. You put 10% down ($33,000) and took out a $297,000 mortgage. It's now several years later. You've been making regular payments and did some renovations.

Step 1: Get your current payoff balance. You log into your servicer's website and request a payoff quote good through the end of the month. It comes back at $261,400.

Step 2: Estimate current market value. Zillow shows $445,000. A local agent's CMA says the realistic range is $430,000 to $455,000, probably around $440,000. You use $440,000 as your working estimate, understanding it could vary.

Step 3: Do the math. $440,000 minus $261,400 equals $178,600 in equity.

Step 4: Calculate your loan-to-value ratio (LTV). Divide your mortgage balance by the estimated value: $261,400 divided by $440,000 is roughly 59.4%. Your LTV is about 59%, which also means you have about 41% equity. Lenders look at LTV constantly, so it's worth understanding both ways to express the same number.

This matters because most lenders won't let you borrow against all of your equity. A typical [home equity loan or HELOC](/heloc-vs-home-equity-loan/) will let you borrow up to a combined loan-to-value (CLTV) of 80% to 85%, meaning you need to keep at least 15% to 20% equity in the home after the new debt is added. Some lenders go higher, but expect a rate penalty for it.

## Why Your Equity Number Can Change Without You Doing Anything

Property values move. That's obvious. But a lot of homeowners don't realize just how quickly their equity can shift in either direction.

In a rising market, you can gain tens of thousands of dollars in equity without making a single extra mortgage payment. The reverse is also true. During the 2008-2012 housing crash, millions of homeowners watched their equity disappear and eventually found themselves underwater, owing more than their homes were worth. The [Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA)](https://www.fhfa.gov/) publishes a House Price Index quarterly, which tracks national and regional price trends if you want a data-driven read on what's happening in your area.

Your equity also builds slowly at first through regular mortgage payments, because of how amortization works. In the early years of a 30-year mortgage, the vast majority of each payment goes toward interest, not principal. On a $300,000 loan at 7%, roughly $1,750 of your first payment goes to interest and maybe $250 goes toward reducing your balance. That ratio flips gradually over time, but it's a real reason why equity builds slowly in the early years of a loan.

Property taxes and any unpaid HOA assessments can become liens on your property in some states, which would also affect your net equity in a practical sense. It's happened to clients I've worked with who had overdue assessments they didn't know had been liened.

## What You Can Actually Do With This Number

Knowing your equity is useful in a few specific ways.

If your LTV has dropped below 80% because your balance has fallen or values have risen, and you're still paying private mortgage insurance (PMI), you may be able to request cancellation. Under the Homeowners Protection Act, servicers are required to automatically cancel PMI when your loan balance reaches 78% of the original purchase price, but you can request cancellation at 80% and potentially save yourself months of premiums. Worth a phone call.

If you're considering a cash-out refinance or a home equity loan, your equity position determines how much you can borrow and at what terms. And if you're unsure about what products make sense for your situation, talking to a [HUD-approved housing counselor](https://www.hud.gov/i_want_to/talk_to_a_housing_counselor) is a genuinely good move, especially if you're considering using your equity to consolidate debt or fund a major expense. They're not salespeople, which is exactly why I recommend them.
